Activity Guide: Zeno, Escher, and the Limit

An exploration of infinity and paradoxes from "Gödel, Escher, Bach"

Your Mission

The concept of a **limit** is the foundation of calculus, but before it was a formal mathematical tool, it was a deep philosophical puzzle. In this activity, you will read a famous dialogue by Douglas Hofstadter featuring Zeno of Elea, a philosopher famous for his paradoxes about motion and infinity.

Your goal is to connect the philosophical ideas in the dialogue to the concrete mathematical problems you solve in calculus. You will work in your small group to answer questions before and after reading the text.

Part 1: Pre-Reading Discussion

Instructions:

Before you read the dialogue, discuss the following questions with your group. The goal is to get you thinking about the strange nature of infinity.

  1. The Halfway Paradox: Imagine you want to walk to a wall. First, you have to walk half the distance. Then you have to walk half of the *remaining* distance. Then half of that new remainder, and so on forever. Will you ever physically touch the wall? Why or why not?

  2. Infinite Sums: Can you add up an infinite number of positive numbers and get a finite answer? For example, consider the sum S = 1/2 + 1/4 + 1/8 + 1/16 + ... What number does this sum seem to be getting closer and closer to?
  3. What is a Paradox? In your own words, what is a paradox? Can something seem logically true and false at the same time?

Part 2: The Reading

Instructions:

Now, read the provided dialogue between Achilles, the Tortoise, and Zeno. As you read, pay attention to the arguments Zeno makes and how Achilles and the Tortoise react to them. Think about how Zeno's "proof" that motion is impossible relates to your pre-reading discussion.

Part 3: Post-Reading Discussion

Instructions:

After everyone in your group has finished reading, discuss the following questions together.

  1. Zeno's Argument: In your own words, what is Zeno's argument for why Achilles can never catch the Tortoise? What is the "flaw" that Achilles senses but can't quite name?
  2. The Escher Connection: The characters discuss M.C. Escher and see an "impossible" flag. Escher was famous for creating art that played with paradox and infinity. How does the idea of an impossible object in art relate to the idea of a logical paradox in philosophy?
  3. The "One Great Idea": The Tortoise suggests Zeno only has "one Great Idea" that he exploits in different ways. What is that core idea?

Part 4: Connecting Paradox to Calculus

Instructions:

Zeno's paradoxes highlight a problem that calculus solves with the formal concept of a **limit**. The paradox lies in an infinite process, but the limit gives us the finite result. Look at the following calculus problems and discuss how they represent the same core idea as Zeno's paradox.

Connection 1: The Limit from a Table

In Zeno's paradox, Achilles takes an infinite number of steps to get closer and closer to the Tortoise. How is that infinite process similar to the values of x in this table getting closer and closer to -2? Even though x never actually equals -2 in the table, what can you say about the value that g(x) is approaching?

Connection 2: The Limit from a Graph

In the dialogue, Zeno's argument focuses on an infinite number of points in time *before* Achilles catches the Tortoise. He never talks about the exact moment they meet. How is this similar to the "hole" in the graph of the function h(x) at x = -1? The function isn't defined at that exact point, but the limit tells us what value the function is approaching from both sides. Discuss the difference between the value h(-1) and the value of limx→-1 h(x).
