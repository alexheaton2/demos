Unit 2: An Introduction to Derivatives

Secants & Average Rate of Change

The average rate of change is the slope of the secant line between two points on a curve. It tells us the function's average "speed" over an interval.

Metaphor: Think of a road trip. Your average speed is the total distance divided by the total time. This doesn't tell you your speed at any specific moment, but it gives you the overall rate of change for the entire trip.

Graphs of Functions & Their Derivatives

The derivative graph, f'(x), tells you the slope of the original function, f(x), at every point. Where f(x) goes up, f'(x) is positive. Where f(x) has a peak or valley, f'(x) is zero.

Metaphor: Imagine hiking on a mountain, f(x). The derivative, f'(x), is a device that tells you the steepness of the trail. It shows a positive number going uphill, zero at the peak, and a negative number going downhill.

Derivative as Slope of Tangent Line

The derivative at a single point gives us the instantaneous rate of change. This is the slope of the tangent line—the line that just "touches" the curve at that one point.

Metaphor: Back to the road trip. The instantaneous rate of change is what your speedometer reads at a specific moment. It's the "slope" of your journey at that exact instant.

The Limit Definition of the Derivative

This is the mathematical machine we use to find the exact slope of the tangent line. We find the slope of a secant line over an interval 'h', then shrink 'h' to zero.

Metaphor: Think of a high-resolution camera zooming in on a curve. As you zoom in infinitely close (letting h → 0), any small piece of the curve starts to look like a straight line. The limit definition is the lens of our camera.

Estimating Derivatives from Tables

When we only have a table of values, we can't find the exact derivative. Instead, we get a good estimate by calculating the average rate of change between the two closest points.

Metaphor: Imagine you only have photos of a car's location at 1:59 PM and 2:01 PM. You can't know its exact speed at 2:00, but you can calculate its average speed between those two times to get the best estimate.

Differentiability: The Visuals

A function is not differentiable at a point if its graph has a sharp corner, a cusp, a vertical tangent, or a discontinuity (a break). Essentially, the graph must be "smooth" and unbroken.

Metaphor: Think of a roller coaster track. It's differentiable everywhere you can stay on the track smoothly. You can't differentiate at a sharp corner (you'd fly off), a vertical drop, or a gap in the track.

Differentiability: The Algebra

For a piecewise function to be differentiable at a point, two conditions must be met: 1) The function must be continuous at that point (the pieces must meet). 2) The derivatives of the pieces must be equal at that point (they must meet smoothly).

Metaphor: Imagine two sections of a garden hose. For them to be "differentiable," you first need to connect them (continuity). Then, you need to make sure they are perfectly aligned so there's no kink (equal derivatives).

The Power Rule

The Power Rule is our first major shortcut for finding derivatives. For any function f(x) = xⁿ, its derivative is f'(x) = nxⁿ⁻¹. You bring the power down as a multiplier and then subtract one from the original power.

Metaphor: The Power Rule is like a "bring down and reduce" machine. An exponent goes in, gets copied down in front, and then the original exponent gets reduced by one.

Power Rule with Negative & Fractional Powers

The Power Rule works for all real number exponents, not just positive integers! The key is often rewriting expressions like 1/x³ as x⁻³ or √x as x¹/² before applying the rule.

Metaphor: Think of this as putting on the right "Power Rule glasses." You might see a fraction or a root, but with your glasses on, you see it as x to a power, and the rule becomes easy to apply.

Basic Differentiation Rules

Derivatives have helpful properties. The derivative of a sum is the sum of the derivatives. Also, you can pull constants out in front before differentiating.

Metaphor: This is like doing laundry. You can wash a big pile of clothes all at once, or you can separate them and wash them in smaller loads. The result is the same. The derivative of a sum is the sum of the derivatives.

Finding Tangent Lines of Polynomials

We can now combine our skills. Use the power rule to find the derivative (the slope), plug in the x-value to get the specific slope, find the y-value, and then write the equation of the tangent line.

Metaphor: This is like building something from a kit. You have all the pieces: the point-slope formula is your blueprint, and the derivative is your power tool to find the exact slope you need.

Derivatives of Sine & Cosine

The derivatives of sin(x) and cos(x) have a special, cyclical relationship. The derivative of sin(x) is cos(x), and the derivative of cos(x) is -sin(x).

Metaphor: Think of a Ferris wheel. Your vertical position is sin(x) and horizontal is cos(x). Your vertical speed (derivative of sine) is fastest at the middle (where cosine is 1) and zero at the top (where cosine is 0).

Derivatives of eˣ and ln(x)

Two more special functions! The function eˣ is its own derivative, making it unique. The derivative of the natural logarithm, ln(x), is simply 1/x.

Metaphor: eˣ is the "perfect growth" function; its rate of growth is always equal to its current size. ln(x) represents "diminishing returns"; its slope is very steep at the beginning but constantly decreases as x grows.

The Product Rule

To find the derivative of a product of two functions, f(x)g(x), you can't just multiply the derivatives. The rule is: f(x)g'(x) + g(x)f'(x).

Metaphor: Imagine a rectangular field whose length and width are growing. The total change in area comes from two parts: the extra strip from the length growing, plus the extra strip from the width growing.

The Quotient Rule

For the derivative of a fraction of functions, f(x)/g(x), we have a special rule: (g(x)f'(x) - f(x)g'(x)) / [g(x)]². A common mnemonic is "Low D-High minus High D-Low, over Low-Low."

Metaphor: The Quotient Rule is a specific, unchangeable recipe. You must follow the steps exactly in order, including the subtraction, or the final dish won't come out right.

Derivatives of Other Trig Functions

The derivatives of tan(x), cot(x), sec(x), and csc(x) can all be derived by rewriting them in terms of sin(x) and cos(x) and then applying the quotient rule.

Metaphor: Think of a family tree. `sin(x)` and `cos(x)` are the parents. All other trig functions are their children. To find the children's derivatives, you go back to the parents and use the quotient rule.
