Unit 1: Computing Limits

A presentation of examples.

Limits by Direct Substitution

The Idea: The simplest way to find a limit! If a function is "well-behaved" (continuous) at a certain point, the limit as you approach that point is simply the function's value *at* that point. Just plug in the number and see what you get.

Example: Direct Substitution

When Direct Substitution Fails

The Problem: Sometimes, plugging in the value gives you an undefined result, like division by zero. If you get a non-zero number divided by zero, the limit does not exist (it might be heading towards $\infty$ or $-\infty$).

Example: Limit Does Not Exist

Limits of Piecewise Functions

The Idea: A piecewise function follows different rules for different parts of its domain. To find the limit at the point where the rule changes, you must check the limit from both the left and the right. If they are the same, the limit exists!

Example: Piecewise Function

Limits by Factoring

The Idea: If direct substitution gives you the indeterminate form $\frac{0}{0}$, it's a clue that there's a "hole" in the graph. Often, you can factor the numerator and denominator to cancel out the term causing the zero, effectively "plugging" the hole so you can then use direct substitution.

Limits Using Conjugates

The Idea: When you get $\frac{0}{0}$ and there's a square root involved, factoring might not work. The trick is to multiply the numerator and denominator by the conjugate of the expression with the root. This often cleans up the expression, allowing you to cancel a term and find the limit.

Limits of Trig Functions

The Idea: For most points, finding the limit of a trigonometric function is as simple as direct substitution. Sine, cosine, tangent, etc., are all continuous everywhere they are defined. Just plug in the angle (in radians!) and evaluate.

Limits Using Trig Identities

The Idea: What if direct substitution on a trig function gives you $\frac{0}{0}$? It's time to use your knowledge of trigonometric identities! Rewrite the expression using identities (like $\sin^2(x) + \cos^2(x) = 1$) to simplify it, cancel terms, and find the limit.

Example: Using Trig Identities

Drawing Conclusions from Direct Substitution

The Idea: The result of direct substitution tells you what to do next. If you get a number, you're done! If you get a non-zero number over zero, the limit doesn't exist. If you get $\frac{0}{0}$, you have more work to do.

Next Steps for Indeterminate Forms

The Idea: Getting $\frac{0}{0}$ means "keep going"! Look at the function's form to decide your strategy. Is it a rational function? Try factoring. Does it have a square root? Try conjugates. Is it trigonometric? Use identities.

Strategy for Finding Limits

The Idea: Think of finding limits as a path. You always start with direct substitution. The result of that first step tells you which path to take next, leading you through different algebraic techniques until you finally arrive at the answer.
