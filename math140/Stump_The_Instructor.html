Activity Guide: The "Stump the Instructor" Challenge

Objective

Your goal is to work collaboratively to find or create a challenging calculus problem within the scope of this course. You must fully understand the problem and its complete solution to effectively present it. The ultimate goal is to "stump" your instructor or TA with a problem that requires deep thinking and a strong command of calculus concepts.

Remember: This activity is designed to enhance your mastery of the subject, explore the limits of the concepts you've learned, and sharpen your mathematical communication skills. You will need to submit written evidence of your group's entire process.

Part 1: Explore & Brainstorm

Your Goal:

To identify interesting or challenging areas of calculus and then dive deeper into specific problem types within those areas.

A. Foundational Brainstorming

  • Review Course Topics: Look through your syllabus, notes, and textbook. Discuss which major topics (limits, differentiation, integration, series, etc.) your group found most interesting or difficult.
  • Identify Areas of Interest: A great "stumping" problem often lies at the intersection of several concepts or in a tricky application of a single one. Select at least five broad topics to consider.

B. Deepen Your Understanding with AI

Use an AI assistant as a tool to explore variations and uncover complex scenarios. This will help you move from a broad topic to a specific, challenging problem idea.

Sample Prompts for an AI Assistant:

  • "Show me some advanced applications of the Mean Value Theorem in engineering."
  • "What are some common misconceptions or tricky scenarios when dealing with Taylor series?"
  • "Provide examples of complex related rates problems that involve multiple variables or constraints."
  • "Explain L'HÃ´pital's Rule and then provide a problem that looks like it uses this rule but is actually a trick question."

Part 2: Find or Create Your Problem

Your Goal:

To either find a suitably difficult existing problem or to design your own original problem.

Option A: The Search

Look for challenging problems in advanced textbooks, online university problem sets, or mathematical competition archives (e.g., Putnam Competition). The problem should require insight or a clever combination of techniques, not just be a standard exercise.

Option B: The Creation

Design your own problem. This is often more rewarding. Start with a tricky concept and add a twist:

  • Combine multiple concepts.
  • Set the problem in an unusual real-world context.
  • Introduce a parameter and ask how the solution changes.
  • Create a problem where a common method fails.

Part 3: Master the Solution

Your Goal:

To ensure every group member can solve the problem cold and explain the solution clearly and confidently.

  • Individual Work First: Each member should attempt to solve the problem alone. This ensures everyone engages deeply with the material.
  • Collaborate on the Solution: As a group, work through the solution step-by-step. Compare your individual attempts and debate the most elegant or efficient method. Understand the "why" behind each step.
  • Consider Edge Cases: Are there other valid ways to solve the problem? Does it have constraints or edge cases that need to be considered? What if you changed an initial condition?

Part 4: Present Your Challenge

Your Goal:

To present your problem clearly and professionally, and to learn from observing an expert's problem-solving approach.

  • The Presentation: Clearly and confidently state your finalized problem to the instructor or TA.
  • Observe and Learn: Pay close attention to how the instructor approaches the problem. What strategies do they use? How do they organize their work? This is a valuable learning experience.
  • Be Ready to Guide: If the instructor gets stuck, be prepared to offer a small, pre-planned hint.
  • Post-Attempt Discussion: After the attempt, be prepared to present your group's complete, elegant solution and discuss the key challenges of the problem.

Your Submission Checklist

Submit a single document from your group containing tangible, written evidence of your entire process. The document should be organized and include the following items:

  • Brainstorming List:

    A list of the 5+ broad calculus topics you considered, with a brief justification for each.

  • AI Exploration Log:

    The prompts you used with an AI assistant and a summary of the most promising ideas you discovered.

  • Problem Development:

    If you created your own problem, show its evolution from a simpler idea to the final complex version. If you found the problem, cite your source.

  • Final Problem Statement:

    A clean, polished, final version of the problem you presented.

  • Detailed Solution Key:

    A complete, step-by-step solution written with enough clarity for a classmate to follow. Include notes on any alternative solution paths or interesting edge cases your group discussed.
