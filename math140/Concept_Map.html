Activity Guide: The Calculus Concept Map

Your Mission

Your goal is to work with your group to create a large, visual, and artistic representation of the most important ideas from a unit of study. Your final "concept map" must follow two core rules, which are explained in detail below:

  • You must choose one overarching visual theme to connect all the concepts: a Landscape, a Map, or a Storyline.
  • Every concept on your map must be represented by its three "pillars": Words, Pictures, and Formulas.

This is a creative activity designed to help you synthesize your knowledge and build a powerful study tool. For inspiration on which concepts to cover, you can review the unit overview on Khan Academy. For example, you could focus on Unit 2: Differentiation, or choose another unit your class has covered.

Part 1: The Three Pillars of Understanding

For every major concept your group includes on your map, you must represent it in three distinct ways. This ensures a deep and multi-faceted understanding.

Words

A clear, concise explanation of the concept in your own words. What is the core idea?

Pictures

Include multiple diagrams, graphs, or drawings that visually represent the concept. How can you "see" the idea in different ways?

Formulas

List all key mathematical formulas or rules associated with the concept. Are there multiple important equations?

Part 2: Examples in Action

Here are three examples of how to apply the "Words, Pictures, Formulas" framework to concepts from Unit 2: Differentiation.

Concept: Average Rate of Change

Words

The average speed or slope between two distinct points on a function's graph. It describes how a function changes on average over an interval.

Pictures

A graph showing a curve with a straight secant line connecting two points, with the "rise" and "run" labeled.

Formulas

(f(b) - f(a)) / (b - a)

Concept: The Product Rule

Words

The rule for finding the derivative of a product of two functions. It's a sum of two terms where you take the derivative of one function at a time.

Pictures

A diagram showing two functions, f and g, splitting apart, with a prime symbol (') alternating between them to show who "gets derived" in each term.

Formulas

d/dx[f(x)g(x)] = f'(x)g(x) + f(x)g'(x)

Part 3: Connecting the Concepts

The most important part of this activity is to show how the concepts relate to each other. Your final poster should not be a disconnected list of ideas; it should tell a story. Use your chosen creative theme to visually link everything together.

The Landscape

Draw a landscape where concepts are features. Is the concept of a limit a river that flows into Derivative Lake? Is the Chain Rule a winding mountain path? Are continuity and differentiability different types of bridges?

The Map

Design a city or world map. Maybe there's the "District of Differentiation" with landmarks like the "Product Rule Power Plant" and the "Quotient Rule Quarry." How do the roads connect these places?

The Storyline

Create a comic strip or a hero's journey. Does your character need to learn the Power Rule to climb a wall, or use the concept of the derivative to find the fastest path down a mountain?
