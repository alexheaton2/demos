The Secret of the Cubic Formula

A Calculus Group Activity on Geometric Reasoning

Part 1: Thinking Geometrically

In calculus, you learned the product rule for derivatives. One of the most intuitive ways to understand it is by thinking about the area of a rectangle. If a rectangle has a width u and a height v, its area is A = uv. If you change the width and height by tiny amounts, du and dv, the change in area is shown by the three shaded rectangles in the diagram below.

The total change in area is \( dA = u(dv) + v(du) + (du)(dv) \). In calculus, we imagine these changes are infinitesimally small, so the corner piece \( (du)(dv) \) is negligible, leaving us with the product rule: \( dA = u(dv) + v(du) \). You may have seen this written in Leibniz notation as \( \frac{d(fg)}{dx} = f \frac{dg}{dx} + g \frac{df}{dx} \), or in prime notation as \( (fg)' = f'g + fg' \).

Before the invention of modern symbols like x and y, mathematicians thought of every product as a literal area, and every product of three terms as a literal volume. This geometric thinking leads to some fascinating history and surprising solutions, as you'll see in the video.

Part 3: Group Discussion & Written Output

Now, get together with your group. Discuss the following questions and write down your collective answers.

Question 1: Cardano's Geometric Solution (Recall)

The video shows Gerolamo Cardano's geometric solution to the specific problem \(x^3 + 6x = 20\). He imagined a large cube of side length t, and broke it down into smaller volumes. As a group, try to reconstruct this argument on paper. What was the key insight that allowed him to solve for x? If you get stuck, it's okay to look back at the video. The goal is to understand each step!

Question 2: Visualizing Cubic Roots (Interactive)

Cardano's method works for "depressed" cubic equations of the form \(x^3 + px = q\). We can find the solution by finding the root of the function \(f(x) = x^3 + px - q\). Use the interactive plot below to see how the solution changes as you vary p and q.

Part B: The video mentions the "casus irreducibilis," a frustrating case where the formula requires complex numbers even though there are three simple, real-number solutions. This happens when p is negative. Try to find a negative value for p (and some value for q) that results in the function having three distinct real roots (crossing the x-axis three times).

Question 3: The Birth of a New Number (Synthesis)

The video's stunning conclusion is that the cubic formula, not the quadratic, forced mathematicians to invent imaginary and complex numbers. Why was \( \sqrt{-1} \) unavoidable for solving cubics, even when the final answers were real numbers? What does this tell you about the nature of numbers and problem-solving?

Please make sure your group's written answers are neat and clear. This document will be the evidence of your effort, thinking, and discussion during this activity.
