Introduction: What a Second Derivative Tells Us

In single-variable calculus, the second derivative, f''(x), gives us crucial information about a function's concavity. It measures the change in the slope of the tangent line. A positive second derivative means the function is concave up (like a cup holding water), while a negative one means it's concave down, like a hill.

But what if our function depends on more than one variable, like f(x, y)? This could represent the temperature on a metal plate or the altitude of a landscape. We can combine the second partial derivatives to create the **Laplacian**, denoted by ∇² (pronounced "del-squared"). The Laplacian tells us how the value of a function at a point compares to the average value in its immediate neighborhood.

Harmonic Functions (∇²f = 0)

These represent a state of equilibrium. The function's value at any point is the exact average of its neighbors. Think of a perfectly stretched soap film, which minimizes its surface area.

The Laplacian Eigenvalue Problem (∇²f = λf)

This describes systems where a function's curvature is proportional to its value. This feedback loop creates standing waves, like on a vibrating drumhead, where λ is related to the frequency.

Step 1: The One-Dimensional Case

In one dimension, the Laplacian is simply the second derivative: ∇²f(x) = f''(x).

  • A 1D Harmonic Function satisfies f''(x) = 0.
  • The 1D Laplacian Eigenvalue Problem is f''(x) = λf(x).

Exercise 2

Let's try to build a non-trivial solution to the 1D eigenvalue problem. For solutions to oscillate (like a wave), the eigenvalue λ must be negative. Let's write λ = -k² for some positive constant k. Our equation is now:

f''(x) = -k² f(x)

This equation states that a function's concavity is always the opposite sign of its value. Let's reason through what the graph of such a function must look like.

  1. Pick a point on the graph where the function has a positive value (i.e., it's above the x-axis, so f(x) > 0). According to the equation, what must be the sign of the second derivative f''(x)? What does this tell you about the concavity of the graph at all points where it is positive?
  2. Now consider a point where the function has a negative value (f(x) < 0). What is the sign of f''(x) here, and what does that mean for the concavity of the graph whenever it is below the x-axis?
  3. Where must the inflection points of the graph be located? (Recall that an inflection point can only occur where f''(x) = 0). Use the equation to determine where this happens.
  4. Sketch a function that obeys these three rules, starting at x=0 with f(0)=0 and an initial positive slope. Describe the shape that you are forced to draw. Does it resemble a common function from trigonometry?

Step 2: Stepping Up to Two Dimensions

For a function f(x, y), the Laplacian is the sum of the second partial derivatives: ∇²f = ∂²f/∂x² + ∂²f/∂y². A function is harmonic if ∇²f = 0, meaning its concavity in the x-direction and y-direction perfectly cancel each other out, creating a saddle shape or a flat plane.

Step 3: Generating Harmonic Functions with Complex Numbers

There's a deep and surprising connection between harmonic functions and complex numbers. Any analytic function of a complex variable z = x + iy has real and imaginary parts that are harmonic functions of (x, y).

For example, F(z) = z² = (x + iy)² = (x² - y²) + i(2xy). The real part is u(x,y) = x² - y² and the imaginary part is v(x,y) = 2xy. You showed u(x,y) is harmonic in Exercise 3, and you can verify that v(x,y) is as well. This works for any integer power n!
