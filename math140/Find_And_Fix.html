Find & Fix Your Trouble Spots

An activity to uncover challenges and learn from each other.

Part 1: Mindset Check-in (5 minutes)

Before we dive in, let's get into a growth mindset. The goal today isn't to be perfect, but to be curious about what challenges you. Take a moment to reflect on these questions:

  • Think of a time you struggled with a new idea (in any subject) but eventually understood it. What helped you finally "get it"?
  • Why is finding a "trouble spot" a good thing for learning?

Part 2: The Hunt for Questions (15 minutes)

Your mission is to find problems that make you pause and think. This is your chance to pinpoint exactly where you can improve.

  1. Go to the Khan Academy AP Calculus AB course.
  2. Choose one of the 8 Units that you want to test yourself on. Pick one you feel a bit shaky on.
  3. Scroll to the bottom of the unit's topic list and start its "Unit Test".
  4. As you work, be a detective. Look for questions that:
    • You get wrong.
    • You get right, but you were guessing or felt unsure.
    • You solve, but you know there must be a better or faster way.
  5. When you find a good "trouble spot" question, take a screenshot or otherwise save the question and its answer/explanation. Keep these handy for the next part. Aim to collect 2-3 questions.

Part 3: Group Collaboration (Remaining Time)

This is where we put the pieces together. By sharing our trouble spots, we can learn from everyone's unique perspective.

The Process:

  1. Share the Challenge: One person starts by sharing their screen to show one of their saved questions. Briefly explain what was tricky about it for you.
  2. Silent Think Time (1-2 minutes): Everyone else silently examines the problem. Think: "How would I start? What do I know? What's the key concept here?"
  3. Teach & Learn: After the quiet time, the floor is open. This is a group effort, not a test. Help each other by:
    • Sharing the first step you would take.
    • Pointing out a key formula, theorem, or concept.
    • Drawing a quick sketch or graph on paper and showing it.
    • Explaining the "why" behind a step, not just the "how".
    • Offering a different way to approach the problem.
  4. Rotate Roles: After a few minutes of discussion on one problem, the next person in the group shares one of their questions. Repeat the process.
